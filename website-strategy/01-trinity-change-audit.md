## 2. Likely Strengths

- **Mission clarity:** Nonprofit branding often carries authentic, people-first language that resonates with families and individuals.
- **Established presence:** Existing domain, any existing SEO equity, and recognition within the regional center network are valuable.
- **Relationship-based tone:** Smaller orgs often communicate warmth that larger agencies lack — this is a real differentiator.
- **California DDS familiarity:** Messaging likely already uses appropriate language (SLS, ILS, ISP, SC, etc.) familiar to regional center staff.

---

## 3. Likely Weaknesses

### Trust & Credibility
- No visible licenses, certifications, or vendor agreements prominently displayed
- No accreditations or regulatory compliance badges (DDS vendor #, liability insurance, etc.)
- Thin or absent team/staff bios — visitors can't assess who will be caring for their loved one
- No Google reviews integration or third-party trust signals

### Lead Generation
- No clear primary CTA above the fold on the homepage
- No intake/inquiry form specific to service type (families, regional centers, individuals receive the same generic form)
- No live chat or callback option
- Phone number not persistent in header across all pages

### Caregiver Recruitment
- Careers page (if present) likely lacks DSP-specific messaging
- No benefits breakdown, pay range transparency, or career path information
- No application tracking or streamlined apply flow
- No employee testimonials or "day in the life" content

### Regional Center / Referral Partner Experience
- No dedicated referral portal or referral-specific landing page
- No downloadable intake packet, capability statement, or vendor overview
- No list of regional centers currently served
- Service coordinator resources likely absent

### SEO & Discoverability
- Likely minimal on-page SEO
- No blog or resource library generating organic traffic
- No local SEO signals (service area pages, Google Business Profile integration)
- URLs likely not optimized (e.g., `/page?id=12` vs `/supported-living-services-california/`)

### Design & UX
- Likely not optimized for mobile (common on older WordPress sites)
- Slow page load (common without performance optimization)
- Navigation may not clearly separate audiences (families vs. referral partners vs. job seekers)
- Visual hierarchy may not guide visitors to the right next step

## 5. What to Retain from Trinity Change

- People-first, dignity-centered language
- Mission-driven framing (even in for-profit context, mission matters to families)
- Any existing service descriptions that are already compliant and accurate
- Any established relationships or logos (regional centers, associations)
- Core service categories (SLS, ILS, community integration)

---

## 6. What to Leave Behind

- Generic nonprofit aesthetic (too modest for a for-profit agency that needs to compete)
- Any outdated compliance language or missing HIPAA/ADA notices
- Weak or missing CTAs
- Any page that doesn't serve a clear audience or business goal
- Cluttered navigation without audience segmentation

## 7. Competitive Positioning Recommendation

The new site should position the agency as:

> **"A California-based, person-centered care agency with the professionalism of a regional provider and the heart of a mission-driven organization."**

Key differentiators to highlight:
- Local California roots + regional center relationships
- Person-centered, individualized planning
- Qualified, trained DSPs and caregivers
- Transparent processes for families, SCs, and referral partners
- For-profit stability with mission-aligned values
